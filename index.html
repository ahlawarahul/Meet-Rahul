Telecom BSS Modernization

Industry

Telecommunications

Business Context

The organization wanted to modernize the technology of a multitude of its business-supporting technology functions, including Customer Frontend, Billing, Finance, Middleware, Fulfilment, etc. The objective was to save on costs to manage these applications, while supporting faster Go to Market (GTM) for new products and services.

Key Challenge

Ability to meet the business-defined timelines to complete the change. The various engineering teams were setup for small-scale changes but not such a large-scale initiative.

Applied Efforts

Setup and manage a central program office and org structure to drive various aspects of the delivery and management of the overall program and change. This included building and facilitating coordination between the various engineering teams (including third-party vendors), product owners/business process owners, marketing, finance and other key functions to ensure necessary and timely collaboration on requirements, change intake, delivery/release planning, architecture & design, various decisions, risks, dependencies, and issues. The program office also conducted necessary reporting across all levels, providing visibility to the leadership and the individual teams on progress and key risks/issues.

Result

Effective program management led to this key initiative meeting its planned timelines as well as reducing the overall expected technology costs by $20M p.a., and the time to release new products by 33%.

Insurance IT Standardization Strategy

Industry

Health Insurance

Business Context

The organization had grown through acquisitions, and was looking to rationalize the technology across various units to standardize and streamline the overall tech footprint while also reducing unnecessary costs of maintaining multiple stacks across geographies.

Key Challenge

Defining the roadmap for refactoring business applications and releasing change to various markets, considering interdependencies with other business functions & supporting applications, user impact, partner impact (e.g. hospitals), operational process impact and overall risk to a business with 12M+ subscribers.

Applied Efforts

This challenge required a real deep-dive into multiple evaluation criteria for each application, gathering various types of data to understand and define the strategy. Looking into 30+ business processes, 20+ systems and 15+ business & technology teams and,
A. At a high level, assessing the strategic risk by gathering business metrics on each application, such as key use cases supported, customer group & market supported, revenue impact, etc.
B. Secondly, defining operational risk, assessing data on aspects such as count of periodic transactions, external (interface) communications, Ops process impact due to change, level of vendor support, and cost to change, etc.
C. Finally, evaluating technical risk, looking at no. of sub-modules per application and rough lines of code, no. of integrated systems, required people skill to change/maintain applications, etc.

Result

Using the 3-layered matrix of information on strategic, operational and technical risk by application/function, key stakeholders were able to decide on a low-risk 'path-to-change'. The modernization program commenced and was eventually completed in phases over a period of 3 years, producing net savings of $10M+ p.a. through the consolidation.

Government Healthcare Solution Design

Industry

Government: Public Sector

Business Context

The State Government Health Department wanted to find an effective solution to allow faster access for physicians and patients to results from critical genetic testing conducted for pregnant mothers. This communication process was paper-based, and results took up to 6 days from being finalized to being received, delaying any follow-up care and counseling critical for mother and child's wellbeing.

Key Challenge

Building a solution that would provide not just a quick way of accessing the patient test results, but was cost effective (given tax dollars are being used), it would be easy to navigate and use, provided the right level of information, would be HIPAA compliant & secure, and scalable to the number of users. Nothing of this scale had been undertaken in the department prior.

Applied Efforts

Creating a solution to meet the needs and challenges required a wholistic approach, from user experience down to architecture. Leading the project team, the approach was multi-fold:
A. User experience was designed working with department officials, but was repeatedly refined with feedback taken from actual hospital physicians, as well as sample patients.
B. An Agile delivery model was utilized to allow ability to be flexible in design while keeping the project under the budget constraints.
C. Healthcare application security experts were used to ensure access and security were properly implemented.
D. Moving away from an on-prem model, a cloud-based hosting model was used for the first time to allow auto-scaling of resources and control infrastructure costs.

Result

When the $5M project went into production, it effectively reduced the results delivery time from 6 to 1 day, and the overall test cycle turnaround time from 10 days to 5. More than 100K users had utilized the portal within 6 months. Additionally, for every paper-based communication replaced by online-results (patient choice), the state stands to save 5 dollars (annual savings of up to $2.5M) from the program.

Customer Support Operations Redesign

Industry

Technology

Business Context

The organization owned a suite of 10 B2B technology product offerings offered to various clients in America & Europe. It wanted to review its Customer Support functions across these offerings given a poor Net Promoter Score (NPS) of 5, and improve it to 7 or higher over a period of 1 year.

Key Challenge

The Customer Support was executed in a disparate manner, with 8 different support teams following a variety of processes, using differing technology platforms and no standardized metrics to monitor or evaluate support quality or customer feedback beyond the NPS.

Applied Efforts

This project required a combination of process redesign and technology standardization to achieve its preset goals:
A. Various data metrics were measured and gathered over a period of 1 month across all the support teams. These included Avg. time to resolve tickets, Avg. ticket backlog, SLAs or SLOs, distribution of tickets from critical to low priority.
B. A deep-dive was done into all the processes, customer experience using the technology and the various engagement channels - phone, chat, email, etc.
C. In addition, shadowing of support associates was conducted to understand where inefficiencies if any could by identified and resolved.
D. Using the data gathered, specific changes were made to set simplified and consolidated processes, and standard metrics for all the support teams. In addition, associates were re-organized to balance the teams according the complexity of product supported and no. of tickets, client importance, etc.
E. The support teams were more closely integrated with the engineering teams to understand new features as they were released, and build a knowledge base for those, thus reducing time to resolve tickets.
F. Finally, teams were migrated to a new support platform which provided a uniform experience to all external users and the support team, as well as making platform maintenance simplified.

Result

Once the new process structures came into effect, a clear improvement in the time to resolve tickets was observed, going down on average by 30%. This combined with the migration to the new platform meant that external users had a better user experience as well (for e.g. being able to use chat which wasn't available earlier for each product supported). A tighter relationship with the engineering teams, and the existence of a knowledge base helped the associates tackle tickets in a more efficient manner. Six months after the 'go-live' the NPS was measured at 8, a clear improvement over the original 5 and the targeted goal of 7.

Inventory Management Platform Enablement

Industry

Healthcare Sciences

Business Context

The organization was small-sized, and wanted to improve its overall lab inventory management processes through the use of technology. Lack of a good platform to manage inventory data and effective reporting caused unpredictable shortages, and hampered the ability to conduct clinical testing and deliver results under contracted SLAs, leading to end-client (hospital org) impact and potential loss of business to competition.

Key Challenge

The organization wanted a solution stood up in a short time (4 months) that would ensure the lab testing delays reduced from the existing 5% down to 2% due to inventory shortages. Not achieving this target would have a direct implication with upcoming renewals for sizeable B2B clients. The existing management processes were almost entirely manual, and not well documented. Lastly, given the organization's small size, cost had to be controlled.

Applied Efforts

With two key targets necessary to be accomplished, i.e. time to deliver and impact on delays, it was important to have an approach that focused on delivering the 'must-haves' vs the 'good-to-haves'.
A. To begin, key processes were documented and captured as requirements first. These laid the foundation of what the platform solution needed to support.
B. A Hybrid-Agile delivery approach was applied to allow a quick start to the implementation as well as to allow for some level of change during the course of implementation but not risking the overall timeline.
C. Business managers and key users were designated as product-owners to ensure the functionality was built not just to meet the high-level targets but also to provide a decent level of usability. They were key participants in user acceptance testing (UAT).
D. Requirements were marked as 'MVP - Minimum Viable Product' or identified as 'Good-to-have' in which case they'd be tackled in the future via a separate project or release, and agreed as such with the stakeholders.
E. Finally, in order to manage the project to the limited budget, an open-source technology was used with an engineering team that was confident with it.

Result

With a keen focus on timing and feature development to drive effective management of inventory, the platform was stood up in 4 months, as targeted. Its functionality provided views into incoming inventory, consumed inventory, rate of consumption and recommendations on re-ordering, etc. Everything was recorded in near-real time through integration with bar code scanners used to track the inventory as it arrived and was consumed. Within 6 weeks, the organization saw a reduction in delays to 1%, beating the target of 2%. With specific add-on changes to the processes and recommendation engines, this was further brought down to 0% eventually.

Central Technology Governance Establishment

Industry

Manufacturing (Foods & Beverages)

Business Context

The organization had operations that were spread globally given its expansion through part acquisition and part organic growth. It wanted the ability to centrally oversee and manage technology procurement requests as well as to be cost-efficient and standardized/consolidated in terms of the technology solutions used globally.

Key Challenge

The business was very large and quite widespread with presence in 7 global 'zones'. Each of these had different processes and models in addition to people & other stakeholders who managed technology procurement. All of these had to be aligned to a common governance model.

Applied Efforts

Standardization of technology management & procurement required the establishment of a central, Technology Governance function. To stand up this function -
A. The org model was identified first, which included a Steering Committee that would be headed by the Chief Enterprise Architect, overseen by the CIO and have POCs from each of the global zones (usually a Director level person).
B. Secondly, interviews were conducted to understand existing procurement processes driving technology purchase decisions, as well as key constraints that drove them (such as budget, category of product, timeliness to procure, etc.).
C. A map of all the technology platforms/solutions used across the globe was built. This was used as a starting point to help stakeholders understand the level of fragmentation and the need to standardize and govern these processes.
D. Using the information gathered, a new process model was defined for the Governance function, taking into consideration the constraints and variations applicable to each business unit, and aligned with the designated POCs on the Steering Committee.
E. Once defined, the POCs then took the new model and work within their zones/units to implement it and provide feedback that would help continually refine it.

Result

There was some level of expected resistance from specific business units given the shift in governance/decision control and level of change to procurement. However, working in a collaborative fashion with the POCs and other stakeholders, the new model was made effective in 3 months. The organization started getting the benefits of the centralized governance in how technology was sourced, driving consolidation and savings of about $3.5M total over the next 2 years.
